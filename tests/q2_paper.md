# Mechanism of mRNA Stabilization by Viral cis-Regulatory Elements and Poly(A) Tail Modifications

## Abstract

The study investigates the stabilization mechanism of mRNA by a cis-regulatory element (CRE) derived from an RNA virus, focusing on poly(A) tail modifications. Previous reports indicate that certain RNA viruses modulate poly(A) tails to evade host degradation. This research aims to identify the CRE responsible for this stabilization, elucidate how it enhances mRNA stability, and determine the host enzymes involved. mRNAs containing the CRE were synthesized via in vitro transcription and delivered into cells using lipid nanoparticles. At 24 hours post-transfection, these mRNAs exhibited a more than 10-fold increase in expression compared to controls lacking the CRE. Nanopore direct RNA sequencing (dRNA-seq) was employed to analyze RNA stability, comparing active and inactive groups based on stabilization outcomes. The active group showed significantly longer poly(A) tails, averaging 120-127 nucleotides, compared to 40-51 nucleotides in controls. Signal analysis revealed the presence of Guanosine residues within the poly(A) tails of stabilized mRNAs, suggesting a "mixed tail" configuration that impedes deadenylation. This modification is hypothesized to involve host enzymes TENT4A/B and ZCCHC14, which may recruit these polymerases to the CRE. The findings highlight a viral strategy to hijack host RNA modification pathways, offering insights into potential therapeutic applications for enhancing mRNA stability in biotechnological contexts. The study provides a foundation for further exploration of host-virus interactions and the development of stable RNA-based therapeutics.

## Discussion

The elucidation of the mechanism by which an RNA virus-derived cis-regulatory element (CRE) enhances mRNA stability through poly(A) tail modifications provides significant insights into viral evasion strategies and host-pathogen interactions. The findings from this study reveal that the CRE facilitates the recruitment of host enzymes, specifically TENT4A and TENT4B, to incorporate non-adenosine residues into the poly(A) tail, thereby enhancing mRNA stability [3], [4]. This mechanism is evidenced by the observed increase in mRNA expression levels, which were more than tenfold higher in CRE-containing mRNAs compared to controls [5]. The nanopore direct RNA sequencing (dRNA-seq) data further supports this by showing distinct differences in poly(A) tail composition and length between CRE-positive and CRE-negative mRNAs.

The incorporation of guanosine residues into the poly(A) tail, as observed in this study, is a novel adaptation that effectively stalls the CCR4-NOT deadenylase complex, thereby preventing mRNA degradation. The ability of the CRE to enhance mRNA stability through poly(A) tail modifications presents potential applications in biotechnology and therapeutic mRNA design.
